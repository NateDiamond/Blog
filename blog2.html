Blog Post #2

What Even Is Freezing Rain?

Today, in classical Ithaca fashion, I woke up to Alexa warning me that it was freezing rain! First of all, how is freezing rain even a thing? Isn't that just snow?



Regardless, I knew I needed to grab an umbrella. Immediately, my mind went to the free umbrella we got on Birthright. My first reaction was temporary relief—now I'll get to stay dry! Then, my mind started to wander. What if it said something "Jew-y" on it? I didn't feel comfortable touting Israeli-themed paraphernalia around campus. But why was that? I have an Optiver umbrella that I sport frequently on rainy days, and that makes less sense given they rejected me!

Does my hesitance to take this umbrella with me say more about the campus climate surrounding Israel or my own insecurity about my Judaism? Honestly, despite issues on campus in the past, I actually believe it is the latter that drove my thought process this morning. Cornell is 20% Jewish, though I was one of the only Jews in my high school of over 1000 students (so maybe a couple of percentage points if I'm being generous). I'm used to being singled out as a Jew without even trying. It is thus habitual for me to try and not draw further attention to this fact.

Here's a question: Would I ever wear a kippah on campus?
The answer—probably not. I'm not talking about at a religious event or a Shabbat dinner. I mean walking between classes in broad daylight.
A) I would never even think to do this because I never did this with my family, and a large part of my Judaism is centered around inherited traditions—though recently I've added a new twist of reducing work and technological use on Shabbat that has never been done in the Diamond household.
B) Even if someone suggested I try it, I would feel uncomfortable. I would be lying if I said I didn't notice "those kids" that wear kippot around campus. This is a prime example of my own biases against Jews (yes, even as a Jew) that I must fight.

I guess in a way, an Israel umbrella is a lot like a kippah. Both cover your head. Both identify you as a Jew. And both make me uncomfortable in certain settings. Fortunately, a wise Birthright tour guide once told me that the best learning is done when you're uncomfortable.
